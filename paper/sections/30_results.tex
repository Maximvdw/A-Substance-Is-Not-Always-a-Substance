\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Data Sources and Integration}
\label{sec:results-datasources}

Integration of the 18 regulatory sources (see Section~\ref{sec:datasources}) yielded a unified dataset of 41,778 regulatory records. Each record was linked to its originating source and enriched with all available chemical identifiers (CAS number, EC number, substance name, and InChIKey where resolvable). The combined dataset spans the full breadth of European regulatory instruments, from broadly scoped REACH registration data (\textit{reach\_registrations}, 25,184 records) to narrowly focused obligation lists such as the SVHC identification list (273 records) and the POPs process list (12 records). The Flemish \textit{sommatie\_stoffen} list contributes a further 1,390 records dominated by substance groups without resolved structures.

\subsection{Chemical Identity Classification}
\label{sec:results-classification}

The rule-based classifier described in Section~\ref{sec:classification} assigned each of the 41,778 regulatory records to one of seven mutually exclusive entity types (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis4a}). The majority — 26,346 records (63.1\%) — are classified as \textit{Molecule}, indicating the presence of a valid InChIKey. The next largest category is \textit{Unclassified} (6,355 records; 15.2\%), comprising entries for which no structural identifier was available and no textual pattern could be matched. \textit{CAS without structure} accounts for 4,854 records (11.6\%); these entries possess a CAS number but no resolvable InChIKey in PubChem. \textit{Analytical parameter} entries — including reaction masses, carbon-range notations, and semicolon-separated mixtures — number 3,436 (8.2\%). The remaining categories are comparatively small: \textit{Mixture} (685; 1.6\%), \textit{Substance group} (86; 0.2\%), and \textit{Regulatory entry} (16; 0.04\%).

  \centering

  \caption{Overall distribution of entity types across all 41,778 regulatory records in the combined ECHA dataset. Molecules (entries with a valid InChIKey) account for 63.1\% of all entries.}
  \label{fig:analysis4a}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:analysis4b} illustrates how the entity type composition varies substantially across individual regulatory sources. Sources rooted in pharmaceutical and pesticide authorisation contexts — ED Assessment (85.5\% Molecule), CLH Process (84.2\%), and the Candidate List (80.6\%) — are dominated by structure-defined entries. In contrast, the \textit{sommatie\_stoffen} list (3.9\% Molecule) and the Restriction Process (29.4\%) contain a high proportion of unclassified or group-type entries, reflecting the broader, less chemically specific scope of those instruments.

\subsection{Linkability Taxonomy}
\label{sec:results-linkability}

Application of the seven-tier linkability taxonomy (Section~\ref{sec:linkability}) to the full dataset reveals that 20,892 records (62.5\%) are fully addressable via a direct InChIKey (tier 1; Figure~\ref{fig:analysis4d}). A further 60 substance group entries could be partially resolved: 15 records (${<}0.1$\%) belong to tier 2 (group with resolvable base compound) and 45 (0.1\%) to tier 3 (group with unresolvable base compound). Together these tiers comprise the structure-addressable portion of the dataset.

The remaining 37.5\% of records are not directly addressable by structure. Of these, 3,869 records (11.6\%) carry a CAS number but no resolvable InChIKey (tier 4); 3,622 (10.8\%) are UVCB substances or undefined mixtures (tier 5); 15 records (${<}0.1$\%) are administrative regulatory entries (tier 6); and 4,977 (14.9\%) are fully unidentified entries with no actionable identifier (tier 7). This last group — nearly one in six regulatory records — represents the most significant barrier to automated cross-list substance matching.

\end{figure}

\subsection{Identifier Mapping Consistency}
\label{sec:results-identifier-mapping}

Of the 41,778 regulatory records, 17,265 carry both a CAS number and an InChIKey and could be assessed for bidirectional mapping consistency (Section~\ref{sec:identifier-mapping}).

In the CAS$\rightarrow$InChIKey direction (Analysis 3a), 17,261 of 17,265 assessed CAS numbers (99.98\%) map unambiguously to a single InChIKey. The remaining four CAS numbers each map to exactly two distinct InChIKeys (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis3a}). These discrepancies arise from entries in which the same CAS registry number has been assigned to structurally related but distinct compounds — most commonly stereoisomeric pairs, hydrate/anhydrous forms, or alternative salt representations recorded in different regulatory dossiers.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Analysis_3a_CAS-InChIKey_inconsistencies_multiple_InChIKeys.pdf}
  \caption{Distribution of the number of distinct InChIKeys per CAS number across the 17,265 assessed records. Four CAS numbers map to two InChIKeys; all others are unambiguous.}
  \label{fig:analysis3a}
\end{figure}

In the InChIKey$\rightarrow$CAS direction (Analysis 3b), 17,063 unique InChIKeys could be assessed, of which 16,869 (98.9\%) are associated with a single CAS number. The remaining 194 InChIKeys (1.1\%) each map to two or more CAS numbers (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis3b}), reflecting the common regulatory practice of assigning separate registry numbers to the same molecule under different trade, salt, or regulatory-context identifiers. These findings confirm that CAS numbers alone are insufficient as unambiguous primary identifiers for cross-list substance linking, and motivate the use of InChIKey as the primary key throughout subsequent analyses.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Analysis_3b_CAS-InChIKey_inconsistencies_multiple_CAS_numbers.pdf}
  \caption{Distribution of the number of distinct CAS numbers per InChIKey across 17,063 assessed structures. One hundred and ninety-four InChIKeys are associated with two or more CAS numbers, indicating registry fragmentation.}
  \label{fig:analysis3b}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Structure Coverage and Linking Ratio}
\label{sec:results-coverage}

Across the combined dataset, 26,346 of 41,778 regulatory records (63.1\%) are structure-defined, i.e.\ possess a valid InChIKey (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis1}). The 20,892 unique InChIKeys thus identified represent the directly linkable chemical space within European regulatory legislation.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Analysis_1_What_is_a_substance.pdf}
  \caption{Overall proportion of regulatory records with (63.1\%) and without (36.9\%) a structure-based identifier (InChIKey) across the combined 41,778-record ECHA dataset.}
  \label{fig:analysis1}
\end{figure}

Structure coverage varies substantially across the 18 individual sources (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis2}), ranging from 85.5\% (ED Assessment) to 3.9\% (\textit{sommatie\_stoffen}). Sources operating within tightly defined pharmaceutical or pesticide authorisation frameworks exhibit the highest coverage: ED Assessment (85.5\%), CLH Process (84.2\%), Candidate List (80.6\%), and SVHC Identification List (79.5\%). The large REACH Registrations dataset, which contributes 25,184 records — more than 60\% of the total — achieves 63.3\% coverage. Sources with the lowest coverage include the Restriction Process (29.4\%), the POPs Process (33.3\%), and \textit{sommatie\_stoffen} (3.9\%), the latter dominated by substance group entries for which individual molecular structures are not specified.

\subsection{Semantic Embedding and Clustering of Non-Structure Substances}
\label{sec:results-clustering}

Silhouette score analysis across $k \in \{2, \ldots, 12\}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis8b}) identifies $k = 6$ as the optimal number of k-means clusters for the sentence-transformer embeddings of the 15,432 non-structure substance names (Section~\ref{sec:clustering}). The silhouette score peaks at $k = 6$ before declining monotonically for larger $k$, confirming that six clusters provide the best trade-off between cohesion and separation.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{Analysis_8b_silhouette_score.pdf}
  \caption{Silhouette score as a function of the number of k-means clusters $k$ for sentence-embedding-based clustering of non-structure substance names. The score peaks at $k = 6$.}
  \label{fig:analysis8b}
\end{figure}

Both the UMAP projection (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis8d}) and the t-SNE projection (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis8e}) show clearly separated cluster regions, confirming that the six groups are semantically coherent. Manual inspection of cluster contents yielded the following labels:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textit{Reaction masses} — defined isomer or intermediate mixtures described by the ``Reaction mass of \ldots'' naming convention;
  \item \textit{Complex UVCB substances} — multi-constituent or variable compositions not reducible to a single structure;
  \item \textit{Petroleum and coal tar fractions} — distillation cuts, refinery streams, and tar derivatives;
  \item \textit{Inorganic compounds and metal salts} — simple inorganic species, oxides, and coordination compounds;
  \item \textit{Trade names, codes, and biological materials} — commercial product identifiers and biological matrix entries;
  \item \textit{Polymers, fatty acids, and surfactants} — polymeric substances, long-chain fatty acids, and amphiphilic compounds.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Knowledge Graph Construction}
\label{sec:results-kg}

All processed data — linkability taxonomy assignments, k-means cluster labels, ClassyFire direct-parent annotations, and ChEBI role annotations — were serialised to a unified RDF Turtle knowledge graph as described in Section~\ref{sec:kg}. The graph integrates substance-level nodes (one per unique InChIKey or substance name), their regulatory source memberships, chemical taxonomy classifications, and biological role annotations. Semantic enrichment via SPARQL CONSTRUCT queries over the merged ChemOnt 2.1 SKOS ontology and ChEBI OWL graph enables downstream SPARQL-based queries across the regulatory, structural, and biological dimensions of the dataset. The output file \texttt{data/processed/rdf/substances\_taxonomy.ttl} constitutes the machine-readable foundation for the priority scoring analysis described in the following subsection.

\subsection{Workload Estimation for Manual Curation}
\label{sec:results-workload}

The \textit{sommatie\_stoffen} regulatory list contains 1,382 substance group entries whose pairwise semantic relationships (subset, overlap, equivalence) require manual expert assessment (Section~\ref{sec:workload}). The total number of unique pairs scales quadratically as $\binom{1382}{2} = 954{,}271$. At a throughput of 100 pair assessments per person-day, the total workload is
\[
  D(1382) = \left\lceil \frac{1382 \times 1381}{200} \right\rceil = 9{,}543 \text{ person-days.}
\]

Figure~\ref{fig:analysis10a} plots the cumulative workload curve, illustrating the quadratic growth as additional groups are added. The marginal cost of each new group at the current list size is approximately $\lceil 1381/100 \rceil = 14$ additional person-days (Figure~\ref{fig:analysis10b}).

\subsection{Composite Priority Scoring}
\label{sec:results-prioritisation}

The composite priority scoring model (Section~\ref{sec:prioritisation}) was applied to 17,518 structure-defined substances with at least one regulatory list membership. Scores combine regulatory breadth ($\text{list\_score}$) with biological hazard evidence from ChEBI ($\text{bio\_score}$).

\subsubsection{Top-Ranked Substances}

Figure~\ref{fig:analysis14a} presents the top~50 substances by priority score. Bisphenol A (2,2-bis(4-hydroxyphenyl)propane; BPA) achieves the highest score of 33 ($\text{list\_score} = 30$, $\text{bio\_score} = 3$), appearing across 12 distinct regulatory sources including the Candidate List, Restriction List, Authorisation Process, and Harmonised Classification List. Dibutyl phthalate ranks second with a score of 29 ($\text{list\_score} = 26$, $\text{bio\_score} = 3$), present in 10 sources. The third-ranked substance, 2-(2H-benzotriazol-2-yl)-4,6-di-tert-pentylphenol (score 27), achieves a high regulatory score through simultaneous listing in eight sources including the POPs list and Authorisation List, but receives no biological hazard score, indicating a gap in current ChEBI role annotations for this compound.
